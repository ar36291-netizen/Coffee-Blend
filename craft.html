How it's made

The decisions
behind the cup.

Roast, grind, and chicory aren't just preferences — they're variables that change what ends up in your cup.

01 / Roast

Heat changes
everything.

Roasting is where the green bean's chemistry becomes flavour. Heat triggers the Maillard reaction — breaking down sugars and proteins into hundreds of aromatic compounds. The longer and hotter the roast, the more origin character you trade for roast character.

Light Roast
Higher acidity, more fruit and floral notes, lighter body. Origin character is most pronounced. Best for Ethiopian and Colombian where natural complexity is the point.
Medium Roast — Most versatile
Balanced between origin and roast sweetness. Acidity mellows, body builds, caramel notes develop. Works across filter, pour-over, and espresso.
Dark Roast
Roast flavour dominates — chocolate, smoke, bitterness. Origin notes largely disappear. Essential for Italian Style; works for South Indian filter where bold body is the goal.
02 / Grind

Surface area
is extraction.

Grinding exposes the bean's interior to water. Finer grinds have more surface area — water extracts faster and more completely. Coarser grinds slow extraction down. The right grind depends entirely on your brew method and how long water contacts the grounds.

Whole Bean
Grind fresh at home. Retains flavour longest — ground coffee goes stale in days, whole beans stay fresh for weeks. Best if you have a grinder and want maximum control.
Coarse Grind
French press and cold brew. Large particle size slows extraction over longer brew times (4 min press, 12+ hrs cold brew).
Medium Grind — Everyday default
Drip machines, pour-over, AeroPress. Balanced extraction at standard brew times (3–4 min).
Fine Grind
South Indian filter and espresso. Very fine particle size enables the slow decoction or high-pressure extraction these methods require.
03 / Chicory

A root,
not a bean.

Chicory (Cichorium intybus) is the roasted root of the chicory plant. It has no caffeine, but when roasted it develops bitter, woody, and slightly caramel-like flavours that complement dark coffee and add body to the brew. South Indian filter coffee has used it for generations.

Why it works in South Indian filter
The filter method produces a concentrated decoction diluted with hot milk. Chicory adds body and bitterness that holds up through dilution — without it, the decoction can taste thin. Traditional ratios run 80:20 to 60:40 coffee-to-chicory.
What it changes in the cup
More chicory = darker colour, thicker body, more bitterness, slightly less caffeine. We let you set the ratio so the final cup is your call.
Only available for South Indian origin. Ethiopian, Colombian, and Italian Style are meant to be drunk as straight coffee — adding chicory works against what makes them worth choosing.
